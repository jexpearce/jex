Example: Progressive Hyperkalemia in Renal Failure

Progression in a Patient with Declining Kidney Function

  • Initial serum K⁺: 4.2 mEq/L (normal range)
  • As GFR drops from 40 to 30 mL/min, K⁺ rises to 4.8 mEq/L
  • Further decline to GFR 25 mL/min → K⁺ reaches 5.3 mEq/L
  • At GFR 15 mL/min → K⁺ rises to 6.1 mEq/L

Effects at the Cellular Level

  • Resting membrane potential progressively depolarizes: -90mV → -85mV → -80mV → -70mV
  • Each potassium value depends on previous concentrations
  • Action potential duration shortens incrementally: 250ms → 230ms → 200ms → 170ms
  • Conduction velocity gradually slows across affected regions

This is a very good example of temporal correlation, where each state depends on previous conditions. This is what the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process models with its "memory" property. Unlike white noise, where potassium would randomly jump between values, the OU process is chosen as it would accurately portray this slow progression of electrolyte disturbance.
