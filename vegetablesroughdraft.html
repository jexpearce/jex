  • The weight of all the vegetables in grams is unfortunately not constant. Instead, the values in the database were sorted by how much the average person would eat as a snack or cook with: (e.g. one onion etc.)

  • Below is the import code and the vegetable subset creation: 

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
fruitvegseafood = pd.read_csv('FruitVeg&Seafood.csv')
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

veg = fruitvegseafood[fruitvegseafood["Category"] == "Vegetable"]

Calorie Distribution in Vegetables: 

calories = veg[["Food and Serving","Calories"]]

sorted_calories = calories.sort_values(by='Calories', ascending = True) 

ax = sorted_calories.plot.barh(0,1, color = "mediumseagreen",title = "Calories in Vegetables")

ax.set_xlabel("Calories")
ax.set_ylabel("Vegetable")

  Celery, Radishes, and Iceberg Lettuce, contain the least amount of calories in per gram, making them a great high volume, low calorie food.

  Sweet Corn is actually a grain, not a vegetable. Mushrooms are also classified as a fungus and Squash is actually a fruit. However, I included them because these foods are usually seen as a vegetable by the general public, and contain a similar nutritional profile to other vegetables.

Vitamin C % RDI in Vegetables: 

vitaminc = veg[["Food and Serving","Vitamin C %"]]

sorted_vitaminc = vitaminc.sort_values(by='Vitamin C %', ascending = True)

ax = sorted_vitaminc.plot.barh(0,1, color = "salmon",title = "Recommended Daily Intake of Vitamin C in Vegetables (%)")

ax.set_xlabel("Vitamin C %")
ax.set_ylabel("Vegetable")

  Fun fact: Broccoli is only second to Kiwi for the food with the highest amount of Vitamin C per gram. Not many people would guess that Brocolli beats Oranges and Grapefruit!

Potassium % RDI in Vegetables: 

potassium = veg[["Food and Serving","Potassium %"]]

sorted_potassium = potassium.sort_values(by='Potassium %', ascending = True) 

ax = sorted_potassium.plot.barh(0,1, color = "teal",title = "Recommended Daily Intake of Potassium in Vegetables (%)")

ax.set_xlabel("Potassium %")
ax.set_ylabel("Vegetable")

Potatoes contain 30% more Potassium than Bananas!

Vitamin A % RDI in Vegetables: 

vitamina = veg[["Food and Serving","Vitamin A %"]]
filteredvitamina = vitamina[vitamina["Vitamin A %"] != 0]
sorted_vitamina = filteredvitamina.sort_values(by='Vitamin A %', ascending = True) 

ax = sorted_vitamina.plot.barh(0,1, color = "goldenrod",title = "Recommended Daily Intake of Vitamin A in Vegetables (%)")

ax.set_xlabel("Vitamin A %")
ax.set_ylabel("Vegetable")
plt.text(65, 0, '(Does not include vegetables with 0 Vitamin A)', fontsize = 10)

  Sweet Potatoes once again reaffirming itself as the nutritional powerhouse, smoking out it's cousin, the Regular Potato in Vitamin A.

  Fun Fact: This Vitamin A isn't actually the same form of Vitamin A shared in meats! This is a form called Beta Carotene, which the body needs a whole lot more of to absorb in comparison to pure Vitamin A (retinol) that is found in animal based foods. Still, Beta Carotene is great for you and especially your skin, but unfortunately not your eyesight, as led on by a popular myth.


Analysis:

  In conclusion, we can see that the Sweet Potato is the all-round nutritional powerhouse of the bunch, with high Vitamin A, Potassium, and moderate Vitamin C. Plus, with the untested variable of price, it's the one of the cheapest vegetables in the UK, second only to the Regular Potato.

As noted before, Broccoli is incredibly high in Vitamin C.

The best low calorie, high-volume vegetables to choose consist of Radishes, Iceberg Lettuce, and Celery. However, if you are looking for a cooking vegetable that actually fills you up, the Squash would be my choice, as it is a good substitute for higher calorie starch-type vegetables.

From these nutrients alone, the Green onion is the Vegetable that lacks the most nutrients, although its low calories certainly compensate.
